The birds have almost started singing again, it seems. The full dawn chorus I mean, not just the liquid laughter of the Brothers MacPuck and the constant chatter of the sparrows. Chou said she heard a thrush this morning.

The other day while working in the bathroom I thought I heard the blackbird. There was a background burble of distant starlings, perhaps the odd little tweeting here and there, the loud tootle of a wren. Then, without my really realizing it was happening, a great poet of the bird world came in and started whisperingly tying it all together, soft little resonant warbles of almost woken dream making the soundscape become a tapestry, become more than a random collection of feathered folk not particularly speaking to each other. A single tiny swell it was, in the soon to come orchestrals of spring.

And then, almost at the same time as I began to notice what was happening, it stopped. I felt the blackbird somewhat embarrassed with himself, saying I’m sorry I don’t know what came over me, the concept of happiness is not meant to spontaneously reoccur for quite some time yet, please just forget I ever spoke.

(Not tomorrow but the next day — nest day, I almoist wrote — that’ll be Brigid’s Day, Imbolc — the reason I mention it is because we’re nearly out of firewood. Don’t know whether to order a load, would be kind of nice to have survived this winter on our own supply, so might try to tough it out. Makes me think about one of the old stories concerning Brigid’s Day and weather divination: if the weather is fine, then winter still has some time left to run — the old hag has made the sun shine, you see, because she needs to go out and get more wood in.)

12:24

I wrote something in my notebook yesterday, about one clear way that Buddhism ‘beats’ Christianity: in being completely up front about respect for all life. I had to wonder what the point of even having a religion was, if it didn’t put that level of care up front?

Today, just now, as I polished off yesterday’s Mackerel, saved from the supermarket dustbin, says Mister Cat, I thought: unless the entire point of Christianity is to concern itself exclusively with the death of the human. What does being kind to animals have to do with that? I know, everything has to do with everything, and yes, there is specifically that, but really? Focus!

An exotic heretic, that’s what Chou called me this morning — said sounded good — as we were talking about something else. Religion crops up enough, it seems. (I love how things as apparently mundane as Portsmouth can sound a little exotic to me now. Stranger in a strange land. Normaldy, quite frankly.)

Anyway, that is definitely one of the issues I’ve had with Christ, and Christianity: its focus on the human, explicitly in such a way as to demean non-human life, or to think its value only in relation to our own. (To separate ourselves from it in order to tell it what it is, basically.)

Same problem I had with the idea of Anthropos, or the archetype of the human. A bit like with interdependent origination, or structuralism: it doesn’t mean anything in itself, only as part of a webwork of extra-human terms, or concepts, or ‘selves’. And those relations can become internal, in a more than dryly-philosophical fashion. Eternal, even. Your essence, your human essence, can buzz like an insect and squawk like a bird and mew like a cute baby mammal and hiss like a snake. Take a peek sometime!

But also there is something honest about Anthropocentrism — or something akin to that concept — and I value honesty greatly. Even honesty which isn’t really honesty, like how nature will make a fly that looks like a wasp, but only enough to put you off, not enough to fool you if you’re really paying attention.

So that is like a weird backdoor that Christianity has, if you really trip on it. A backdoor that is deep down strange inside it. God’s bumhole kind of strange. Strangel strange.

(I once scrolled past a sign saying TEST IF YOU’RE AN ANGEL FOR FREE, but Mummy said no, the doctor guy looked kind of creepy.)

The trick is that, in focusing exclusively on the human, it can humanize all life, if you are weird enough to feel that, or need it. Maybe brave enough to know it. So maybe it has to be YOU that does it, and not Christianity, per se.

In Blake’s poem, The Divine Image, from Songs of Innocence, the image of God is not particularly this human form, some divine more-or-less generic human body, which nonetheless Blake is clearly very fond of — it is Love, Mercy, Pity, Peace.

Ever wondered why Big Cats seem to have those tear drops designed into their very eyes?

Wittgenstein said that if a Lion could speak we wouldn’t understand it. But then we have that word: Roar, and we understand that well enough. Isn’t that kind of a Lion’s word? Wouldn’t it be, even if we’d never seen a Lion not even on TV? I think so. I don’t think you can hear a Lion roar and not at least think you get the gist of it, nor be at least partly on the money. Who says that isn’t language? Who says this isn’t us being Lionized, at least a little bit, even as the Lion inevitably also gets Anthropomorphized?

So Nature isn’t all Love Mercy Pity Peace, I agree. And nor are we. I’m not anyway. Whatever we choose to define us, or define ourselves by, which is what saying human means if it actually means anything more than what never needed to be said (which it doesn’t!) is never the whole of us, never all of it. How else could I be unlike myself some days? Always. How else could any organism catch a disease or develop a pathology?

Can we then, really? Can they?

Humanimalien Being. Or doing. Same thing.

Because what is Love Mercy Pity Peace if it doesn’t apply to what it’s not? Doesn’t come into existence precisely through its own incompleteness? Even in some minuscule way. A way so tiny it undoes the concept of scale.

It seems to come into being by relation, then, but how could the infinite ever be considered proportionate? The Real Infinite. Not the one that can be smaller inside some arbitrary subdivision of the one that can thus be greater, while the infinities themselves presumably go on equaling each other?

Can they?

How could we not?

15:16

Am just in the middle, or the middle of the start, of processing the lightning struck hawthorn. Just going at it with secateurs and saw, not quite no iron (sorry fairies) but no machine at least. Apart from me, naturally.

Anyway, Chou, who had just finished working Dreki in the arena, calls me over, saying two Narcissi — the WBSUL name for our current reigning male pheasant — were fighting. I ran around the back of the house to join her in spectating. Huh, never seen that before, she said, more and more interesting things are starting to happen.

There were two of the fellows chasing each other about, and then coming head to head, dipping down towards each other with their heads and then leaping a little way into the air with a flutter or two of their wings. One was slightly bigger than the other — fatter really — but in fact it was the skinnier fellow who seemed to be getting the better of it.

On and on they went. The skinny one did look a little finer and prouder. Tidier, with a slightly more distinctive appearance, having a lighter, higher contrast wing.

Shall we eat the loser? I asked.

Nooo, she said, we will take him in and cuddle him.

I laughed, he could be the totem bird of Fae Lurk: Narcissus the Loser Pheasant.

Then I realized that would be the name of my pub: The Lazy Pheasant.

It was quite a scene, with her riding mirror in the middle of them, our two Narcissi, and them fighting in front of it, just to the north of Clive, the oak who I was tripping up last year, in which event a Pheasant came into the tree and joined me, becoming the amusingly disturbing prize bird of my imagination; while just to the west I am in the process of chopping down, or up as the case may be, the hawthorn that was split in two by lightning a few weeks ago, a hawthorn who had already appeared in a kind of fiction I wrote a few years back, except as a blackthorn (as well as a ghostly lover or forgotten goddess) in a world that was a little like this one, excepting I had inverted all the directions, so this place’s north was that ones south, etc. Talk about echoes!

I don’t know what we’ll do with the hawthorn yet but I’m happy to be processing it by hand. I kind of hoped I would but thought I would probably be too lazy. Luckily it so happens to be just that kind of a lazy sunday down here at the Lazy Pheasant!

Chin chin!

Good health all.

(Just at the end of their battle the fat one appeared down at the bottom of the little alley of gorse we were stood at the top of, then the victor came through behind him and chased him right up towards us. He turned a few metres before he reached us and sulked off towards the far hedge. Don’t worry mate, I said, it was a good fight and we got a lot out of it!)

17:13

A little shower comes over. Not quite finished with the thorn. Some thought about a jail or punishment room for people who failed to pass The Standard Joke test by laughing. Turns out that is The Standard Joke test, whereupon we see the person in the picture, sat alone at a desk in some forlorn corner, their face frozen in surprised boredom as if they’ve just looked up and seen us, with what they were looking at just moments before on the desk there: the very same picture. The Standard Joke test.

And somehow reflecting back on that whole meaning-before-everything-thing, like a kind of anti-Kantian nothing-in-itself. The reason time flows in a certain direction, as well as the reason it doesn’t necessarily, on reflection, or a kind of reflection, being because of this… whatever it was.

The idea is basically that if you looked at the results of Quantum Physics, say, through the lense of a search for meaning, that is: as if you were looking at an idea and not a kind of ‘reality’ or ‘the roots of matter’ or, say, ‘this world’ (in a knock on wood materialist sense) — then you would, or might, somehow find the results less surprising, and not necessarily in a bad way. This, somehow, gist-wise, is Qualom Aweganix, what Psychedelic Poetry really is and will be in or by the 23rd Century. Like Wayback, or one ov um, said: information theory as a quest for meaning = confusion practice. Tis all gist-wise, purrhahahaps furevz.

(The only other thing I wanted to note was that I would quite like to read that later Kant, the one that people seem a bit more suspicious about, as if he was senile or losing it — which he may well’ve been — I just think that even then it would be worthwhile to try to understand the kind of thing senile geniuses spend their time thinking they’re thinking about, or whatever.)

O.Y. (Oh yeah, the Ungléshan way of translating post scriptus, or whatever P.S. means)
